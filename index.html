For thirty years, I've been following my North Star. Not a career ladder. Not a five-year plan. Just: what's worth doing today? It turns out that's a perfectly reasonable way to build a life.

What am I doing this afternoon?

A few years ago, I was in therapy spiraling about the existential terror of "What will I be doing when I'm 60?" My therapist reframed it: "What are you doing this afternoon?"

That stopped the spiral. This afternoon, I'm working on something interesting. And the afternoon after that. And the one after that. Eventually, something I do some afternoon will lead me to the thing I'll be doing when I'm 60.

Stop catastrophizing about the destination. Build momentum one afternoon at a time. Forward motion without anxiety is its own kind of freedom.

This philosophy shaped how I move through the world—especially after I spent 25 years in corporate consulting doing exactly this. Contract to contract. Problem to problem. Never staying still long enough to get stuck, but staying engaged enough to master each thing before moving on.

Thirty years in motion

1997

Perl CGI came out. My manager told me to figure it out. I built an intranet site for mutual fund analysis. It worked. I liked this more than programming.

25+ years consulting

Contract work. Finance, healthcare, government. No gaps between gigs. At 50, I decided freedom and variety beat stability. Not a ladder person.

Now

Websites weren't the future anymore. AI is. So I'm pivoting. I freelance, annotate data, teach people Canva and AI. But freelancing is "being home with my sad and lonely self"—so I also work the front desk at an acupuncture clinic. Connection. Service. That's the point now.

In corporate, I wanted money and prestige. Boston Harbor. Luxury condo. Now I'm on Cape Cod and my purpose is different: to be of service. The part-time work isn't a side thing. It's the work.

What's next?

Honest answer: I have no idea. And I'm okay with that.

I don't need the next thing figured out. I don't have a five-year plan or a grand mission statement. I know the kind of work that interests me. I know my skills. I know I work best when I have freedom to move between things, and enough variety to stay engaged.

So I'm watching for the next afternoon's interesting problem. It could be web design. It could be AI. It could be something I haven't discovered yet. The point is: I'm paying attention. When the next thing shows up, I'll know.

If you want to talk about web design, AI, technology, or just the philosophy of moving through the world without a master plan—let's talk.

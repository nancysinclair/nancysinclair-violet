For thirty years, I've been following my North Star. Not a career ladder. Not a five-year plan. Just: what's worth doing today? It turns out that's a perfectly reasonable way to build a life.

What am I doing this afternoon?

A few years ago, I was in therapy spiraling about the existential terror of "What will I be doing when I'm 60?" My therapist reframed it: "What are you doing this afternoon?"

That stopped the spiral. This afternoon, I'm working on something interesting. And the afternoon after that. And the one after that. Eventually, something I do some afternoon will lead me to the thing I'll be doing when I'm 60.

Stop catastrophizing about the destination. Build momentum one afternoon at a time. Forward motion without anxiety is its own kind of freedom.

This philosophy shaped how I move through the world—especially after I spent 25 years in corporate consulting doing exactly this. Contract to contract. Problem to problem. Never staying still long enough to get stuck, but staying engaged enough to master each thing before moving on.

Forty years in motion

1986

Stumbled into math and programming. No master plan. Just the next thing.

1992

Master's Degree in Applied Mathematics. (I'm super proud of that.) First job: startup as a client-server developer.

1992–2018

Corporate software. 26 years as an independent contractor. Finance, healthcare, government. No gaps between gigs. I moved constantly because I needed variety. Turns out that's very ADHD. Didn't know that yet.

2018

I'm 50. I've spent half my life in corporate. I quit. No plan. Very impulsive. Also very ADHD (again, didn't know that yet).

2020

I'm 52. Pandemic. Diagnosis. ADHD. And suddenly everything—the job-hopping, the inability to fit into a traditional FTE, the need for variety, the hyperfocus on what interests me—suddenly it all made sense. I wasn't broken. My brain just works differently.

Now

Freelancing. Part-time work with people. Multiple interests. Building a life that works *with* my brain instead of against it. Former corporate full-stack developer, current freelancer with too many ideas.

Following the wave

Perl/CGI to web development to AI. Different decade, same person: watching what's next and having the guts to learn it. I've ridden every wave because I got bored with the last thing and paid attention when the next shiny thing showed up. That's not scattered. That's staying alive.

What's next?

Honest answer: I have no idea. And I'm okay with that.

I'm in my second Saturn Return (ends when I'm 60), which is supposedly a time of massive life upheaval. My therapist reframed my spiral about turning 60 as "What are you doing this afternoon?" and that stuck. So I'm watching for the next afternoon's interesting problem.

I don't need the next thing figured out. I don't have a five-year plan or a grand mission statement. But I do have a North Star: I'm here to be in service to others. I know the kind of work that interests me. I know my skills. I know I work best when I have freedom to move between things, and enough variety to stay engaged.

So I'm watching for the next afternoon's interesting problem. It could be web design. It could be AI. It could be something I haven't discovered yet. The point is: I'm paying attention. When the next thing shows up, I'll know.
